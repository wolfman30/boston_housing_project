Transforming Skewed Continuous FeaturesÂ¶

A dataset may sometimes contain at least one feature whose values tend to lie near a single number, but will also have a non-trivial number of vastly larger or smaller values than that single number. Algorithms can be sensitive to such distributions of values and can underperform if the range is not properly normalized. With the census dataset two features fit this description: 'capital-gain' and 'capital-loss'.

Run the code cell below to plot a histogram of these two features. Note the range of the values present and how they are distributed.

For highly-skewed feature distributions such as 'capital-gain' and 'capital-loss', it is common practice to apply a logarithmic transformation on the data so that the very large and very small values do not negatively affect the performance of a learning algorithm. Using a logarithmic transformation significantly reduces the range of values caused by outliers. Care must be taken when applying this transformation however: The logarithm of 0 is undefined, so we must translate the values by a small amount above 0 to apply the the logarithm successfully.

Metrics and the Naive PredictorÂ¶

CharityML, equipped with their research, knows individuals that make more than \$50,000 are most likely to donate to their charity. Because of this, *CharityML* is particularly interested in predicting who makes more than \$50,000 accurately. It would seem that using accuracy as a metric for evaluating a particular model's performace would be appropriate. Additionally, identifying someone that does not make more than \$50,000 as someone who does would be detrimental to *CharityML*, since they are looking to find individuals willing to donate. Therefore, a model's ability to precisely predict those that make more than \$50,000 is more important than the model's ability to recall those individuals. We can use F-beta score as a metric that considers both precision and recall:

$$ F_{\beta} = (1 + \beta^2) \cdot \frac{precision \cdot recall}{\left( \beta^2 \cdot precision \right) + recall} $$

In particular, when $\beta = 0.5$, more emphasis is placed on precision. This is called the F$_{0.5}$ score (or F-score for simplicity).

Looking at the distribution of classes (those who make at most \$50,000, and those who make more), it's clear most individuals do not make more than \$50,000. This can greatly affect accuracy, since we could simply say "this person does not make more than \$50,000" and generally be right, without ever looking at the data! Making such a statement would be called naive, since we have not considered any information to substantiate the claim. It is always important to consider the naive prediction for your data, to help establish a benchmark for whether a model is performing well. That been said, using that prediction would be pointless: If we predicted all people made less than \$50,000, CharityML would identify no one as donors.

Weakness: -*(a) SVMs may not work well with 'large' datasets because of the quadratic programming process that maps support vectors to higher dimensional space to find the separating hyperplanes, which results in high computational and time cost.

Current application: With untuned parameters the model scored at 83% accuracy and took about 6.4 minutes, which clarified that the temporal cost and accuracy is acceptable, though compared to the other two applied models, it is the slowest.

Question 4 - Describing the Model in Layman's TermsÂ¶

In one to two paragraphs, explain to CharityML, in layman's terms, how the final model chosen is supposed to work. Be sure that you are describing the major qualities of the model, such as how the model is trained and how the model makes a prediction. Avoid using advanced mathematical or technical jargon, such as describing equations or discussing the algorithm implementation.

**Answer:

To provide an intuitive explanation of the gradient boosting algorithm, we start with the basic defintion of an algorithm: a sequence of instructions that tells a computer what to do. A learner is an algorithm that makes inferences from data. Next, the gradient boosting algorithm is an ensemble learner. What is 'ensemble learner'? It is a collection of learners, where each individual learner is 'weak' because it makes inferences that have a high proportion of misclassified donors; in some instances the accuracy of the initial learners are only slightly better than a random guess. The particular class of learner used in the gradient boosting alorithm applied to this particular group of donors are tree classifiers, which are often used (but not required) in a gradient boosting algorithm. A tree classifier makes inferences from a dataset by a series of questions like playing the game 20 Questions, where a question is asked based on a feature (i.e. "What is the donor's education level?"), which results in a Yes or No(True or False), which leads to another yes/no feature question, and so on, with the ultimate end of yes/no(true/false) regarding the donor's salary as above 50K. The tree classifiers in this ensemble are 'weak' because they ask less questions, from 4 to 8 questions. Asking a low number of questions prevents the ensemble of tree classifiers from merely memorizing all the details for every donor in this dataset; if the ensemble memorizes everything about the donors in this one group of donors, it won't be able to accurately classify donors outside the group.

Now we break down the terms 'boosting' and 'gradient'. About 'boosting': the gradient boosting algorithm (in the case of this potential donor dataset)uses a sequence of 'weak' tree classifers where each successive tree slightly "boosts", or increases, its accuracy from its immmediate predecessor tree classifier by weighting the misclassified donors of this previous tree classifier, which refocuses the next tree classifier on those mistakes. So overall the first tree classifier has the weakest performance, the next tree classifier improves its classification score from the weighted mistakes of first, the third improves its score based on the weighted mistakes of the second, and so on until the last tree classifier has a 'strong' performance, or a classification score that is up to an acceptable level of performance. Boosting is enabled by adding the 'gradient' of each previous tree classifier to the next tree classifier. To understand the 'gradient' we have to understand these three concepts: 'function', 'loss function' and 'derivative'. A function maps one or more inputs to one or more outputs. The inputs here are the actual donor salaries and the classification of the salaries from the tree classifiers, whch are inputted into a loss fucntion. A 'loss function' is a function that maps each of these inputs to an output that represents the 'loss' of each learner's predictive power, or the difference between actual donor salary and classified donor salaries. To get the lowest possible ouptput from the loss function to find the best learner, we take the 'derivative' of the loss function. The 'derivative' is a major conceptual tool used in calculus for finding lowest and highest values of functions. Taking the 'derivative' of a function allows us to find the output extremes because when the derivative of a function equals zero it corresponds to one of the lowest or highest values of the function's output. The 'gradient' is the derivative of a given loss function. We take the negative gradient, to get to the lowest possible output from the loss function (if we took the positive gradient that would result in learners with increasingly weaker performance). Overall, there are many tree classifiers (successive trees are like descending gradient steps down a 'hill of error') along the sequence learners, each one better than the prior, ending at one last tree classifier with the highest possible accuracy and lowest error (within the limits of the gradient boosting algorithm).
